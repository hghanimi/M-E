INTERVIEWER (EVAL-04):
Thank you for agreeing to speak with us today. As I explained, your
responses will be kept strictly confidential and will only be used in
aggregate form for our programme evaluation. Please feel free to speak
openly. Can you start by telling me how you first heard about the
Maternal Health Clinic services offered in this area?

RESPONDENT (BENE-KE-117):
I heard about it from a community health volunteer who visited our
village — I think it was in early 2023. She organised a community
meeting and explained that a new programme had started. She said
pregnant women and new mothers could receive free antenatal check-ups,
iron supplements, and safe-delivery education. Before the programme
came, the nearest facility was very far, and many women, including
myself, were delivering at home.

INTERVIEWER (EVAL-04):
You mentioned delivering at home before. How has that changed since the
programme began?

RESPONDENT (BENE-KE-117):
It has changed a lot. I myself have attended four antenatal visits this
pregnancy, which I was told to do by the clinic staff. They use a
tablet — I think the name was KoboCollect — to fill in all my
information. It felt very organised. The nurse said that information
goes to the county health office. I felt like someone was keeping
track of us, and that was reassuring.

INTERVIEWER (EVAL-04):
Could you give us a sense of how many women in your community are now
using the clinic services?

RESPONDENT (BENE-KE-117):
In our village alone it is difficult to count exactly, but at the last
group meeting the community health volunteer said that over 2,800
women from surrounding villages had enrolled in the programme since
it started. For our village specifically, I would estimate around 340
to 400 women regularly attend. The numbers have been growing — more
people have heard about it through the radio announcements.

INTERVIEWER (EVAL-04):
Can you describe any specific changes in how the community thinks about
maternal health since the programme arrived?

RESPONDENT (BENE-KE-117):
The biggest change I have seen is with the men. Before, many husbands
did not want to discuss pregnancy or accompany their wives to the
clinic. The programme has a component specifically for husbands — they
call it the "Partners in Health" session. My husband attended two of
those sessions. He now reminds me of my clinic appointments himself.
There is a general sense that this is a family issue, not just a
women's issue. I think that is important for Gender equality.

INTERVIEWER (EVAL-04):
The programme also has a nutrition component. Have you seen any impact
from that?

RESPONDENT (BENE-KE-117):
Yes. There are monthly cooking demonstrations at the sub-county
community centre. They teach mothers how to prepare nutritious meals
using locally available foods. I learned a lot — things like how
groundnuts and green leafy vegetables are important during breastfeeding.
I have passed this knowledge on to my sister-in-law. Word spreads.

INTERVIEWER (EVAL-04):
Shifting slightly — the programme also works across borders. Are you
aware of any activities that extend beyond Kenya?

RESPONDENT (BENE-KE-117):
I heard from the programme officer that a similar project is running
in Uganda and Tanzania. I do not know the details, but I believe it
uses the same approach — the community volunteer model and the tablet
data collection. She said the results from all three countries would
be combined into one big report for the donor.

INTERVIEWER (EVAL-04):
You mentioned a programme officer. Did she say anything about who is
funding this programme?

RESPONDENT (BENE-KE-117):
She mentioned a large international organisation — I think she said
something like a United Nations health body and a European development
fund. She was careful not to mention specific names but said they had
very strict reporting rules, which is why the data collection with the
tablets is so important. Everything must be recorded precisely.

INTERVIEWER (EVAL-04):
From your perspective, what has been the single biggest achievement of
this programme?

RESPONDENT (BENE-KE-117):
For me personally, it is the feeling of safety. Before the programme,
I was anxious about my pregnancy. I did not know if something was wrong.
Now I have a health card, I have regular check-ups, and I have a number
to call if there is an emergency. I believe the number of mothers who
die during childbirth has gone down in this area — the nurse told us
that the programme has supported more than 15,000 safe deliveries
across all the project sites since inception.

INTERVIEWER (EVAL-04):
That is a significant number. Finally, is there anything you feel the
programme could do better, or any challenges you would like to mention?

RESPONDENT (BENE-KE-117):
The biggest challenge is still transport. Even with the clinic nearby,
some women from remote areas still struggle to come in consistently.
There are also periods when the iron supplement supply runs short and
the clinic has nothing to give. I think if the supply chain could be
improved — perhaps using the same data system to predict when stock
will run out — it would help enormously. I have heard that the
programme uses Power BI dashboards for monitoring. If those dashboards
could be linked to the stock levels, maybe the supply gaps could be
caught earlier.

INTERVIEWER (EVAL-04):
Thank you so much for sharing your experience. Your input is very
valuable to our evaluation. As a reminder, your identity will remain
completely confidential and none of your personal details will appear
in any public report or website.

RESPONDENT (BENE-KE-117):
Thank you. I hope the evaluation helps to improve things for women
who come after me.
